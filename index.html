Summary of the Battle

“Smoke and dust swirled all over the battlefield. The earth shook from the powerful blasts. Tanks struck each other and, having grappled, could not cut themselves loose and fought to the death"

-Pavel Rotmistrov, Soviet Commander

One of the bigger tank battles in history, The Battle of Kursk was an important battle in turning the tide of the war against the Nazis. Also known as Operation Citadel, the battle started with a strategic offense by the German forces, trying to attack the bulge of Kursk by the north and south points. The Soviets (USSR) were aware of the operation thanks to Britain sending them information and prepared for the battle by evacuating citizens, digging over 4,800km of trenches with their help, and acquiring hundreds of thousands of anti-tank and anti-personnel mines.

During the early months of 1943, the battle got delayed because Hitler had wanted to use the new Panzer tanks. After 4 months of preparing, the battle started on July 5th, 1943. The Soviets started off with a good defensive front, however the Germans started to advance quicker when the Soviet counterattack lost most of its steam. The Germans gained 80 km over 8 days on the southern offense.


Over the course of the war, the weather was very intermittent. Thunderstorms came one day and 30-degree Celsius weather the next; creating mud which substantially slowed both armies. On July 12th, one of the biggest tank battles started, with 1,500 tanks participating. The Battle of Prokhorovka. During the battle, the Soviets found a weakness in the new, undefeatable Tiger tanks. They started attacking the sides where the armour was thinner. The battle raged on, and smoke from all the damaged tanks reduced the visibility in the sky to the point where airplanes became unusable. 

The Germans eventually lost their momentum. Using tanks, airplanes, and troops, the Soviets managed to defend their land by launching a massive offensive attack towards the central, northern, and western fronts of the German force. At one point, they outnumbered the Germans 2:1 in every encounter. The Germans were unable to call up reinforcements and with Russian partisans derailing trains behind them, it severely hindered Germany’s retreat efforts. Numbers won out in the end, and the Soviets overran the Germans with their larger army.

The battle waged after the Soviets conquered and took back most of its land. It came to a hesitant close on August 23rd with both sides suffering. Germany’s casualties were low at 200,000 compared to the Soviets staggering 860,000 men dead, injured, sick or lost. It was a full 48 days of gruesome combat.

Battle of Prokhorovka

"The battle lines of both sides had been mixed up with one another. Our tankers had successfully hit the enemy at close range, but they too suffered heavy losses.”

- Wilhelm Res, German Tank Crewman

The Battle of Prokhorovka was one of the biggest tank battles in history, taking place near the small area of Prokhorovka, 87km southeast from Kursk. The battle started on the morning of July 12th. This was the beginning of the Soviet’s offensive strike that decisively put the Soviets in the lead. It was fought on the Prokhorovka bridgehead, which they ended up taking it back from the Germans at the end of the day. 

The Soviets started the battle with a barrage at 8am on July 12th, 1943. Shortly after, both sides were fighting what would be one of the largest tank battles in history. The Soviets started with 5 tank brigades, with Germany quickly prepping their army who were exhausted from prior fighting. The Russians quickly overran the 2nd and 3rd Panzer Regiments. The Russians also launched their air force however the Luftwaffe dominated the airspace that day. The 1st Regiment arrived soon after and headed straight for the fighting after seeing an uncountable number of Soviet tanks.

During the afternoon of the 12th, the Soviets attacked the 1st Regiment again, but were soon destroyed by anti-tank crews. They were facing off very close to each other, almost at point blank with the Soviets suffering massive losses. By the end of the day, the Soviets managed to gain some ground back and the 1st Regiment retreated to build more solid defense lines. Both sides suffered heavy losses with one Tank Corps losing over 60% of their forces during the one-day battle.

How the Battle Changed WWII

The Battle of Kursk was the first battle that successfully stopped a German offensive before achieving its goal. It was a big victory for the Soviets, and they even gained some ground after the battle was finished. They were underdogs with inferior weapons and technology, however they had significantly more soldiers. The Soviets lost more men and resources than Germany but made up by being a larger country with considerably more manpower than Germany. This became known as last major offensive the Germans launched on Russia, during World War 2.

Technologies Used

T-34

The T-34 is the Soviet’s main tank that was used in the Battle of Kursk and played a major factor in the Battle of Prokhorovka. The Soviet’s built 35,467 from 1940-44, and their mere numbers outplayed the slightly more sophisticated Panthers and Tigers that they were up against in the Battles. The T-34s were lacking in long range power but made up in close combat with powerful shots that could penetrate the German tanks while surrounding them and striking from all sides.

Panzer V & VI (Panther & Tiger)

Nazi Germany recently finished putting their finishing touches on the Panzer V Panther & Panzer VI Tiger when the Battle of Kursk started. They were the main tanks involved in the battle. The newly engineered design could shoot Soviet tanks up to 2km away. The Tiger specifically was a tank that was so well engineered that the Allies had to come up with better tanks to combat them. It plowed through the Allies, consistently destroying 10-19 tanks before being destroyed. This helped tremendously in the Battle but were eventually overwhelmed by the Soviet T-34s sheer numbers.

Junkers Ju 87 Stuka

400 dive-bombers from the Spanish Civil War made their way to the Battle of Kursk with some upgrades. The Ju 87 was a key factor for the Germans in the air. Destroying ground vehicles and tanks were one of its specialties, capable of holding a 550-pound bomb as well as an anti-tank cannon. Even though they managed well in the air, the Ju 87 very little protection anywhere around its body and couldn’t manage without backup from tankier planes.

Ilyushin Il-2

The Ilyushin Il-2 is the second-most produced aircraft to date, with over 36,000 made during WWII. The Soviets claimed that they were a deadly ground-attack fighter and played an important role during the Battle of Kursk, however many are speculating about their claims. They were equipped with two anti-tank cannons, three machine guns, and up to 1,300 pounds of bombs. However deadly they were, they were a tank in the air with a reinforced plane tip and armour encasing the entire body of the plane, except for the rear cockpit which was shoddily added on and ultimately resulted in more deaths.
